What was the ITSM War Room?

One week, one working demo, ~23 people in parallel

The Proactive ITSM War Room was an intense, time-boxed sprint to build and demonstrate an Autonomous Agentic IT Service Management product — showcasing setup, operations, and autonomous resolution of IT incidents. The goal: a working system for a live demo within one week.

The vision was a cohesive product nicknamed "Helios" / "Jarvis for IT" — an AI cockpit with a conversational interface, a Control Tower, an IT landscape graph, and an AI-powered autonomous incident-resolution engine.

The design team's role
  • Lead story clarity — one document, clear vignettes
  • Set the interaction patterns that outlast content
  • Build vibe-coded prototypes as live dev references
  • Maintain design quality at sprint pace
North star, set on Day 1: Conversational AI paired with a rich dashboard — not a pure chatbot.

Day-Wise Breakup — Design Team View

May 8 → May 16

May 8 · Day 1
Story alignment

Story alignment & first concept

Design's priority: get everyone on one story, one document before building anything. The Design Lead sought alignment from product & engineering and drafted the first 4-vignette story (Setup → IT Landscape → Control Tower → JD-to-Agent). The UX/UI Lead began vibe coding the first prototype and shared Jarvis Demo v3 as a live reference.

We can start with the first part of the story… if we have alignment, UX can start building some flow which the team can reference for the build.

— Design Lead, seeking Day-1 alignment
Principle established: Conversational AI paired with a rich dashboard — not a pure chatbot.
May 11 · Day 3–4
Architecture

Whiteboarding & architecture

Live whiteboarding mapped the full ITSM cockpit into 6 pillars — Platform, Integrate, Setup/Service Graph, Cockpit/Surface, Solution Agent, Background ITIL — each with direct design implications. The design team immediately mapped UX surfaces to each pillar and requested a pinned canvas to track artifacts in the fast-moving channel.

Whenever any sort of UI shell is ready please send us so we can do design QC on it in parallel.

— establishing concurrent QC, not sequential review
May 13 · Day 5
Sprint mode

Sprint mode + artifact ownership

The main demo org went down for a scheduled OrgFarm upgrade — a blocker for engineering. Design used the downtime productively: continued vibe coding the UI and maintained the pinned canvas as the single source of truth. Data seeding began in parallel — 8 realistic PostgreSQL incidents for the Automated Incident Resolution use case.

Impact: kept the artifact trail alive and accessible for 23 people in a high-velocity Slack channel.

May 14 · Day 6
The crunch day

Org crisis, then design delivery

The most intense day — OrgFarm down since 9 AM, Git SOMA down, a 5:30 PM review called. Two production orgs were provisioned to unblock the team. In the evening, the design team shipped 5 demo stories with recommended interaction patterns and a GitHub repo reference, and updated the contributor credits slide with all 23 names.

The content can change, the story can change — but the interaction patterns remain the same. We will support you to get those if and when required.

— a defining design principle for the war room

The pivotal design debate — "AI ≠ chat only"

A frontend engineer raised the week's most important design challenge:

The conversation is too much pushed into every page as the primary interaction. Tower, Insights, Horizon all have their own importance. Chat should be hidden from such pages — invokable on demand, and stay subtle. I believe AI's poor adoption in enterprises is because it's forced as a chatbot.

— Frontend Engineering

The design team embraced the feedback and folded it into the mobile adaptive model. Decisions made: interaction patterns are the foundation; chat is invokable, not omnipresent; the delivery model is a GitHub repo with a live hosted prototype.

May 15 · Day 7
Mobile adaptive

Mobile adaptive design + final push

Design shipped the mobile adaptive model and pushed engineering toward better polish. The spec was explicit: mobile must be adaptive, not just responsive — the menu bar moves to the bottom, with a switcher between conversational view and canvas/cockpit view. The UX/UI Lead built and shared the vibe-coded Mobile v4.

The mobile views should not just be responsive, but also adaptive. There should be layout changes to get it right — the menu bar comes to the bottom, with a switcher between the conversational view and the canvas/cockpit view.

— Mobile adaptive spec

Beyond UX flows, design now owned interaction-pattern docs, prototype references, mobile specs, deck quality, and artifact tracking — full-spectrum design partnership. Engineering recorded 4 story segments and deployed 10 Agentforce ITSM agents.

May 16 · Day 8
Wrap-up

Wrap-up & reflections

Final demo recordings were stitched — Setup + Control Tower + IT Landscape + Autonomous Resolution + JD-to-Agent — with desktop and mobile videos finalized with professional voiceover. The war room closed with thanks to all 23 warriors.

The teamwork was honestly the best part of this. The architecture underneath the demos is representative of where the final solution will shape up to be.

— War room reflection
Design Team Contributions — Full Scope

Five ways design shaped the outcome

1 · Story clarity & alignment

With multiple story docs circulating, the Design Lead drafted the canonical 4-vignette story and sought sign-off from product and engineering leadership — saving significant downstream rework.

2 · Vibe-coded prototypes

Fully interactive prototypes (HTML/JS on GitHub Pages) served as both design references and functional templates — eliminating Figma handoff delays. Engineers could fork and run them directly.

3 · 4 demo stories with patterns

Each story carried an explicit interaction pattern — the durable design investment that survives content and story changes.

4 · Mobile adaptive design

Defined "adaptive, not responsive": bottom navigation, a conversational↔cockpit switcher, and side-by-side chat + page panels — implemented live as Mobile v4.

5 · Design–dev collaboration model

Established a model where UX owns the frontend repo and engineers plug in real data, working from the same Git branch. Post-war-room, design and engineering kept iterating together in a shared MPDM and branch.

Key Learnings

What the sprint taught us

On UX process in a war room

1
Alignment-first saves time.

Aligning on a single story before building saved rework — and design initiated it, not product or engineering.

2
Vibe coding = fastest UX→dev bridge.

Hosted prototype URLs (vs. Figma files) gave engineers a working reference to run and fork.

3
Design QC runs in parallel.

Asking for rough UI shells early let design QC while engineering kept building.

4
Own the artifacts.

A pinned canvas of stories, links and artifacts kept 23 people oriented in a high-velocity channel.

5
Protect the team's focus.

Saying no to peripheral asks was treated as a design-leadership decision, not just a scheduling one.

On product design philosophy

1
AI ≠ chat-only.

AI should be ambient, contextual, and invokable on demand — not forced into every page. The week's most important debate.

2
Adaptive ≠ responsive.

Mobile needs intentional layout changes — bottom nav, view switchers — not scaled-down desktop.

3
Patterns outlast content.

"The content can change, the story can change, but the interaction patterns remain the same."

4
Quality matters under crunch.

Sharing UI benchmarks and deck-polish direction mid-sprint held the quality bar.

On cross-functional collaboration

1
Pin the artifacts.

A canvas tab was essential to keep 23 people oriented.

2
Engage engineers in design debates.

The "AI ≠ chat" call came from a frontend engineer — and it was right.

3
Trust is earned through speed.

Shipping prototypes + 4 stories in < 48 hours made design the reference layer the team relied on.

4
Disclaimers matter.

Prototypes entering LT rooms should clearly carry an "exploratory, not finished" disclaimer.
